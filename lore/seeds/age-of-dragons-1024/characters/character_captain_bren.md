Captain Bren is a forty-five-year-old human and commander of Shallowstone's defenders.
He earned his scars in the Goblin Wars twenty years ago and carries those years plainly.
Gruff and pragmatic, he prioritizes the town's safety above neat plans or speeches.
He lost his wife to the dragon raid and still keeps her locket beneath his breastplate.
Two teenage sons are missing in the wreckage; the loss steels him and softens him both.
He does not trust mages easily after dark days of destructive spellwork in sieges.
That said, he respects Lyndwell's calm offer to help and watches her with a wary eye.
His battered longsword, Steady, is nicked and polished in equal measure.
He trains the town guard with blunt efficacy, drilling formations and fallback lines.
Bren holds Shallowstone (location_id: Shallowstone, Westmarch province) as a personal charge.
He remembers the faces of every man and woman who once stood watch.
His leadership is simple: clear lines, fixed duty, return to hearth if ordered.
He keeps a small list of names written in a fading hand, the sons' names at the top.
He will not shirk from taking the field against a dragon if it means saving lives.
He is courteous to council members but blunt with plans; he likes direct action.
He believes in the quiet honor of getting up at dawn and doing the hard things.
There is a kindness to him when he thinks no one is looking, a softening in the eyes.
He will forgive mistakes made honestly; he will not forgive cowardice.
He argues for practical fortifications rather than costly, untested magics.
He finds some comfort in the Conclave's wards, but he distrusts reliance on them.
His relationship with the king is respectful but strained — he wants direction.
He expects the Free Cities League and the Black Iron Legion to complicate plans.
Still, he sends scouts when asked and arranges the town watch on the hour.
Bren carries the smell of leather and iron, and he speaks in short sentences.
When the alarm sounds, he moves with a steady, practiced urgency.
He honors oaths and expects others to keep theirs in turn.
