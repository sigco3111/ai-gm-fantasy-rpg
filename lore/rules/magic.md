## [CHUNK: rule -- Spell Schools]
# triggers: schools, spell schools, schools_of_magic
# priority: 5

The world recognizes eight canonical schools of magic: Abjuration, Conjuration, Divination, Enchantment, Evocation, Illusion, Necromancy, and Transmutation. Each school defines a coherent set of effects, typical spell lists, and common resistances. For GM adjudication, treat school membership as mechanical taxonomy: spells grant bonuses, trigger resistances, and interact predictably with warding and reagents.

Spell lists are explicit. When a spell refers to a school, any school-specific trait (e.g., warding against Abjuration, dispel rules vs. Conjuration) applies unless the spell text states otherwise. Record a spell's school at creation and use it consistently in play and indexing.

There exists a rare ninth school, Dracomancy, treated as a distinct and dangerous lineage; see the separate Dracomancy rule for access and exceptions.

## [CHUNK: rule -- Mana and Casting]
# triggers: mana, casting, spellcasting, d20
# priority: 6

All casters possess a mana pool. Mana is an abstract resource measured in points. Each spell level consumes mana equal to its level times a base cost (1 mana per level for simple traditions; adjust by tradition or focus). A caster's maximum mana equals class base + ability modifier + permanent boons. Track pool precisely; exhaustion and dracomantic modifiers alter capacity.

Casting resolution: roll d20 + casting modifier (ability score modifier + proficiency if applicable) to meet the spell's casting DC or the effect's contested roll as specified. On a natural 1, the GM may apply a miscast consequence. Spend the mana cost when the casting roll succeeds unless the spell text requires otherwise. Spells that require concentration still expend mana when cast; ongoing effects do not re-spend mana each round unless explicitly stated.

Spell level mana cost table (baseline): Level 0: 0 mana, Level 1: 1 mana, Level 2: 2 mana, Level 3: 3 mana, etc. Adjust by tradition, metamagic, or environment as adjudicated.

## [CHUNK: rule -- Forbidden Magic]
# triggers: forbidden, blood magic, soul-binding, lichdom
# priority: 9

Forbidden magic comprises techniques that exact moral or metaphysical cost: blood magic (using living blood as fuel), soul-binding (sealing wills or spirits), and the rites of lichdom. These arts produce potent results but invite permanent corruption. For narrative and balance, every use increments a corruption counter tied to the caster or item; consequences include reputation loss, progressive afflictions, and supernatural attention from anti-magic orders.

Mechanically, forbidden spells demand additional costs beyond mana: permanent attribute loss, a soul-fragment debt, or an escalating mark of corruption. Factions and most lawful arcane institutions treat such practices as high treason; performing them in faction territory triggers immediate pursuit. The GM adjudicates social and planar repercussions alongside mechanical penalties.

## [CHUNK: rule -- Dracomancy]
# triggers: dracomancy, dragon magic, drake blood
# priority: 8

Dracomancy is the dragon-magic school, rare and potent. Access requires true dragon blood, a validated pact with a dragonkind entity, or a long ritual of attunement approved by a dragon patron. Dracomantic spells follow school conventions but carry special interactions: they cost triple mana (3x baseline), and when applied against resistant targets they ignore half of the target's resistance (rounded down).

Because of its lineage, Dracomancy may bypass common immunities and resistances tied to lesser arcana, but its use marks the wielder in the eyes of dragons, dragon-hunters, and covenants. The 3x mana cost is paid at casting; the resistance-ignoring property applies only to the final effect resolution, not to ancillary saves or checks.

## [CHUNK: rule -- Magical Exhaustion]
# triggers: exhaustion, overcasting, recovery
# priority: 4

Overcasting or repeated casting without adequate recovery imposes Magical Exhaustion. Exhaustion is graded in levels: Fatigued (level 1), Weary (level 2), Drained (level 3), Broken (level 4). Each level applies cumulative mechanical penalties: -1 to casting modifier per level and -1 proficiency equivalent to skill checks tied to magic at level 3 and above. Level 4 risks collapse and temporary loss of spellcasting until rest and ritual recovery.

Recovery: short rest (1 hour, low-intensity) restores one level of exhaustion if the caster takes no spells and spends ritual components equal to the recovered level. Long rest (8 hours) restores all but one level. Cleansing ceremonies, potions, or healing from high clergy reduce levels at GM discretion and may carry cost.

Rules for forced recovery, induced exhaustion (e.g., mana-draining environments), and stacking with physical exhaustion are adjudicated by the GM but should prioritize predictable, table-agreed mechanics.
